If you're working in the natural sciences, sooner or later you will have to learn how to typeset in [<span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span>](http://www.latex-project.org/). There are a number of <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span> editors out there, but I'm a bit old fashioned and have a profound dislike for obese IDEs, so I usually stick to my coder's editor of choice (lately [Sublime Text 2](http://www.sublimetext.com/2)). However, there are some tools that have become a fixed part of my workflow, and for good reasons.

## Mendeley

I don't have a strong preference for [Mendeley](http://www.mendeley.com/) over or any other article management system (such as [Papers](http://www.mekentosj.com/papers/)), other than that it's free. But the one feature your reference management should have is automatically generating a `.bib` file containing all your articles. Put that file on your `PATH` and reference any paper from any article you're writing.

## Handwriting to <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span>

VisionObjects have a great demo that instantly [converts your handwriting to <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span>](http://webdemo.visionobjects.com/equation.html?locale=default) formulas. It works with an HTML5 canvas, so you can use it on your tablet as well.

![VisionObjects LaTeX recognition](http://media.tumblr.com/tumblr_lyx6saZB071qahh0k.png)

## Equations in presentations

You can do all your presentation slides in <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span> using the [Beamer package](https://bitbucket.org/rivanvx/beamer/wiki/Home), but unless your slides are packed with formulas I'd hardly recommend doing so. If you just need to insert a formula every now in then, [LaTeXIT](http://www.chachatelier.fr/latexit) is an incredibly useful little tool for OS X: it's a graphical front to <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span> that renders your equation into the application window, from where you can just drag and drop it into Keynote or Powerpoint to insert it as a PDF.

![LaTeXiT](http://www.chachatelier.fr/latexit/images/dragdrop.png)

<small>(Image copyright lies with Pierre Yves Chatelier)</small>

An online alternative with the same feature set is [CodeCog's equation editor](http://www.codecogs.com/latex/eqneditor.php), however it occasionally comes up with, eh, non-standard interpretations of your equations.

## Throw a <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span> party

Well, you knew that sooner or later some slippery innuendo would come. Anyway, [ShareLatex](https://www.sharelatex.com) is a collaborative <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span> editor. Think [EtherPad](openetherpad.org) with integrated <span class="texhtml" style="font-family:cmr10, LMRoman10-Regular, Times, serif;">L<span style="text-transform: uppercase; font-size: 70%; margin-left: -0.36em; vertical-align: 0.3em; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.15em;">a</span>T<span style="text-transform: uppercase; margin-left: -0.1667em; vertical-align: -0.5ex; line-height: 0; margin-right: -0.125em;">e</span>X</span> compiler and syntax highlighting. Free.
